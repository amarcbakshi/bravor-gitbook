# Theory of Change

## The Causal Chain

Professional Capacity → Infrastructure Gap → Matching Mechanism → Organizational Capacity → Movement Strength → Democratic Resilience

Each link in this chain has evidence behind it. The theory is not aspirational — it traces a direct pathway from an individual attorney's 10 hours to a measurable increase in an organization's ability to defend democratic institutions.

## Context: The Infrastructure Gap

The pro-democracy movement possesses enormous latent human capital. Over 2 million Americans are willing to volunteer in skilled, fractional capacities.<sup>1</sup> Skills-based volunteering searches have grown 300%+ since 2020.<sup>2</sup> But this capacity remains systematically inaccessible because no infrastructure connects supply to demand.

Current workarounds are ad hoc and leaky: personal networks, Slack channels, email chains, conference introductions. These channels work for the well-connected and fail for everyone else. The result is a movement operating at a fraction of its potential — not for lack of will, but for lack of plumbing.

## Intervention: The Matching Layer

Bravor provides the missing infrastructure through three mechanisms:

**1. Discovery** — AI-enabled onboarding surfaces what both sides have and need, creating a structured marketplace where none exists. A professional's expertise becomes findable. An organization's needs become visible.

**2. Matching** — Algorithmic routing connects skill-specific supply to need-specific demand at speed. A contract review request reaches an immigration attorney in hours, not the weeks it takes through personal networks.

**3. Trust** — Verification on both sides (professional credentials, organizational vetting, feedback loops) creates the confidence layer that enables strangers to work together effectively.

## Change Pathway

**Short-term (6–12 months):**
- Professionals contribute 2,600+ hours of skilled labor to vetted organizations
- Organizations close capacity gaps that would otherwise go unaddressed
- Match quality improves as the system learns from feedback
- Estimated $750K in professional services value delivered

**Medium-term (12–24 months):**
- Network effects accelerate: successful contributors recruit peers; satisfied organizations post more needs
- White-label partnerships with institutional partners expand reach beyond direct recruitment
- The matching engine becomes a proprietary dataset of civic skill supply and demand
- Estimated $7.5M+ in capacity delivered across 50+ organizations

**Long-term (24–48 months):**
- Bravor becomes embedded infrastructure — the default way skilled professionals find civic contribution pathways
- Institutional partnerships create self-sustaining distribution
- Revenue from organization subscriptions and white-label licensing reduces grant dependency
- Estimated $50M+ in cumulative capacity delivered

## Assumptions and Risks

| Assumption | Evidence | Risk if wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Professionals will complete tasks once matched | Catchafire reports 85%+ completion rates for scoped projects<sup>3</sup> | Platform becomes a signup-only graveyard; mitigate with task scoping and first-match optimization |
| Organizations can articulate discrete needs | Pilot interviews confirm this with coaching; some orgs struggle | Build need-articulation tools into org onboarding |
| AI matching improves with data | Standard ML pattern; validated in commercial marketplaces | Slow improvement requires more human curation in early phases |
| White-label partners will adopt | Bar associations already run pro bono programs they struggle to staff | Slower adoption means longer path to scale; direct recruitment compensates |

## Proof Points

- **Legal Services Corporation** reports 92% of low-income Americans receive insufficient legal help — confirming massive demand for legal volunteer capacity<sup>4</sup>
- **Democracy 2025 coalition** mobilized 800+ legal experts within weeks of formation — proving rapid professional mobilization is possible when infrastructure exists<sup>5</sup>
- **Skills-based volunteering** generates 3–5x the value of traditional volunteering per hour contributed<sup>6</sup>
