# Customer Segmentation

## Platform Structure

Bravor is a **two-sided matching platform** connecting professional contributors (supply) with frontline organizations (demand). Both sides are "customers" in the product sense — but the platform is built for professionals first, because supply unlocks demand.

| Segment | Role | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Skilled Professionals | Supply side. Contribute expertise in discrete, time-bound tasks. The product is designed for their constraints first. | Primary |
| Frontline Organizations | Demand side. Post needs and receive matched volunteers. Value grows with supply quality. | Secondary |
| Institutional Partners | Distribution channel. Bar associations, law clinics, media networks license the infrastructure. | Tertiary |

## Primary Segment: Time-Constrained Professionals

Attorneys, media strategists, financial analysts, designers, and academics with 5–20 hours per month of available capacity. They want to contribute their deepest professional skills to causes they care about — but every existing pathway is either too generic, too demanding, or too hard to find.

Three non-consumer archetypes explain why they haven't engaged:

* **"I can't find anything real"** — They've searched for pro bono opportunities matching their expertise and availability. Nothing connects skill-specific supply to mission-specific demand.
* **"I don't have time for a commitment"** — Existing platforms require sustained multi-month engagement. They have 10 hours this month and different availability next month.
* **"I don't trust what's out there"** — No verification mechanism tells them whether an organization is legitimate, effective, or worth their time.

Evidence of demand: 28.3% of Americans volunteered in 2023.<sup>1</sup> Skills-based volunteering searches have grown over 300% since 2020.<sup>2</sup> Over 120,000 professionals are estimated to be available for fractional pro-democracy contribution in law, media, design, and finance alone.<sup>3</sup>

## Secondary Segment: Frontline Organizations

Nonprofits with 3–50 staff fighting for democracy, voting rights, immigration justice, reproductive rights, and related causes. Chronically understaffed. They need specific expertise — a contract reviewed, a financial model built, a press strategy developed — but can't afford to hire consultants and can't find qualified volunteers.

95% of nonprofit leaders report burnout and capacity concerns.<sup>4</sup> 74.6% report unfilled vacancies.<sup>5</sup> The justice gap shows 92% of low-income Americans receive insufficient legal help.<sup>6</sup>

## Tertiary Segment: Institutional Partners

Bar associations, university legal clinics, media rights nonprofits, and philanthropic networks that want to mobilize their own member communities. They license Bravor's matching infrastructure to create branded volunteer ecosystems within their networks — what we call the white-label model. This is both a distribution channel and a revenue stream.

## Sequencing

* **Phase 1 (MVP):** Legal-first wedge. Attorneys and paralegals matched with litigation-heavy nonprofits. Highest stickiness, clearest value, fastest proof.
* **Phase 2 (Expansion):** Add media, finance, and design professionals. Expand org base to 50+ organizations across cause areas.
* **Phase 3 (Scale):** White-label partnerships launch. Institutional distribution compounds network effects.
